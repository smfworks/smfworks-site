# Epictetus Audio Script — Middle School (Ages 11–14)
## Chapter 6: Practice Makes Strong

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Respectful, slightly more mature, still accessible

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. We have come to the final chapter of our journey with Epictetus. We have learned about the boy with no name, the circle you control, the unbreakable center, the two arrows, and the guest at the banquet.

But all of these ideas are useless if we do not practice them.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that philosophy is not a subject you study once and then forget. It is training. It is a way of life. It is something you do every day, the way an athlete trains or a musician practices scales.

Think about it. You do not become brave by reading a book about courage. You become brave by doing brave things, even small ones. You do not become patient by thinking about patience. You become patient by practicing patience when you are impatient.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a girl named Priya.

Priya was twelve, and she wanted to be calmer. She had a quick temper. When someone teased her, she snapped back. When she made a mistake, she berated herself. When things did not go her way, she sulked.

She decided to practice the Circle of Control. Every day for a month, she wrote down one thing outside her circle and one thing inside it. At first, it felt forced. But slowly, she started noticing the pattern before her temper could flare.

One day, a classmate made a rude comment about her shoes. The old Priya would have fired back. The new Priya paused. Was the comment inside her circle? No. Was her response inside her circle? Yes.

She walked away. It was not dramatic. Nobody applauded. But Priya felt different. She had practiced something until it became part of her.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
That is what Epictetus meant by practice. Small choices, repeated, until they become who you are.

Here are three ways to practice this week:

**First, make a Seven-Day Training Plan.** Pick one idea from this series. Try it every day for a week. Keep a small log of what happens.

**Second, make a Trigger List.** Write down five situations that usually make you react strongly. For each one, write a short sentence you can say to yourself in the moment.

**Third, make a Personal Philosophy Card.** On an index card, write five rules for yourself inspired by Epictetus. Carry it for two weeks. Then revise it based on what you actually needed.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You have done something important by listening to these stories. You have met a man who lived two thousand years ago and discovered that his wisdom is still alive today.

But the story is not over. The most important part is what you do next.

Will you practice? Will you come back to these ideas when life gets hard? Will you be the kind of person who chooses wisdom, even in small moments?

I hope so. Because the world needs more people like you.

This is George, wishing you courage, wisdom, and an unbreakable center.

Goodbye, philosopher.
