# Epictetus Audio Script — High School (Ages 15–18)
## Chapter 5: The Guest at the Banquet

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, philosophical, emotionally honest

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to talk about one of the most counterintuitive ideas in Stoicism: the idea that everything is on loan. It sounds bleak at first. But it may be one of the most liberating ideas you will ever encounter.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus compared life to a banquet. You did not earn the invitation. You did not prepare the meal. You did not build the hall. You are a guest. A good guest receives what is offered with gratitude and leaves without complaint when the feast is over.

This applies to everything: your body, your possessions, your relationships, your status, even your life.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a young man named Daniel.

Daniel was eighteen, and his grandfather was dying. They had been close. His grandfather had taught him to fish, to drive, to stand up for himself. Now the old man lay in a hospital bed, growing thinner and quieter each day.

Daniel was angry. He did not want to say goodbye. He wanted to demand more time. He felt life was being unfair, taking something that should have been his.

One evening, a hospice chaplain sat with him.

"Your grandfather was never yours to keep," she said gently. "He was on loan. And the fact that he was a gift does not make the goodbye easier. But it can make the thank-you more honest."

Daniel did not want to hear it. But over the following days, he began to understand. His grandfather had been a gift. Every fishing trip, every conversation, every awkward lesson in driving — all of it had been given for a time, not forever.

When he sat by the bed and held his grandfather’s hand, he stopped fighting the goodbye long enough to say thank you.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that treating things as loans does not mean loving them less. It means loving them more carefully. When you know something is temporary, you pay attention to it. You do not postpone the important conversations. You do not take the people you love for granted.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, make a Gratitude Scan.** List ten things in your life right now that you would miss if they were gone. Be honest. Include at least one person, one place, one ability, one ordinary daily pleasure, and one thing you usually complain about.

**Second, write a Loan Agreement.** Pick three things that matter to you — one possession, one relationship, one status or role. Write each as if on loan. How does that change how you treat each one today?

**Third, write a Memento Mori Letter.** Write a short letter to yourself from the perspective of someone near the end of life. What would they tell you to stop worrying about? What would they tell you to pay attention to?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The banquet of life is beautiful. The people, the moments, the small joys — all gifts. A good guest does not grab everything and refuse to leave. A good guest says thank you, enjoys the meal, and walks out the door with dignity when the feast is over.

That is what Epictetus wanted us to learn. Not to love less, but to hold more lightly.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
