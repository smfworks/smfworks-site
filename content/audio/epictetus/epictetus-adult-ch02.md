# Epictetus Audio Script — Adult
## Chapter 2: The Circle You Control

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, reflective, direct, no self-help padding

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are examining the foundation of Epictetus's ethics: the dichotomy of control. Some things are up to us. Most are not. The quality of our lives depends more on this distinction than on almost anything else.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
At the opening of the *Enchiridion*, Epictetus writes that what is up to us includes our judgment, impulse, desire, and aversion. What is not up to us includes our body, property, reputation, public office, and the actions of others.

That is the whole framework. Simple to state. Difficult to live.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a woman named Helena.

Helena was fifty-three, and she had chaired a philosophy department for twenty years. She had shaped the curriculum, hired junior faculty, defended the major through budget cuts, and built a small but respected Stoicism reading group.

Then a new administration arrived. Humanities programs would be "right-sized." The department would be merged into religious studies. Her position would be reduced. Her life's architecture was being dismantled.

For days, Helena spiraled. She replayed every decision. She blamed herself. She catastrophized about money, reputation, age, and the future. She had taught the dichotomy of control for decades. Now she had to live it.

One morning, she sat in her garden and drew a circle. Outside it: the administration, the budget, the board, the market forces, the housing market, her wife's medical condition, her daughter's tuition. Inside it: her response, her integrity, her care for colleagues, her clarity, her daily choices.

The circle was small. But it was entirely hers.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have told her what she already knew: the more energy you spend on the outside, the less you have for the inside. The Stoic does not stop planning or caring. The Stoic stops worshipping control.

Adult life makes this especially hard. Mortgages, children, employees, aging parents, communities — these responsibilities are real. But precisely because they are real, you cannot afford to waste your finite energy on what you cannot command.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, the External Audit.** List five things you currently believe are necessary for your happiness. For each, ask: Is it fully within my power? Have I over-invested? If the answer is no, write a sentence releasing it from the category of "necessary."

**Second, Control Mapping.** Choose a current stressor. Create two columns: "Within my power" and "Not within my power." For each external item, write what an internal response would look like. Be specific.

**Third, the Weekly Review.** For one week, before bed, ask: "Did I spend more energy today on what I control or on what I do not?" Just notice.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The circle of control is not a magic solution. It will not save your department, your marriage, or your health. But it will return to you the energy you have been spending on outcomes you cannot command.

And that energy is the only thing that can actually meet your life well.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
