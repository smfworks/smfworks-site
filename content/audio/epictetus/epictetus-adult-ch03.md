# Epictetus Audio Script — Adult
## Chapter 3: The Unbreakable Center

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, reflective, direct, no self-help padding

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are talking about loss, identity, and the part of you that does not have to break.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that externals can be damaged, lost, or taken away. This is not pessimism. It is realism. Your body will age. Your possessions will change hands. Your relationships will end or transform. Your status will rise and fall.

But the part of you that chooses, that learns, that endures, that loves — that part does not have to break unless you surrender it.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a woman named Sarah.

Sarah was a pediatric surgeon, respected by colleagues and trusted by patients. At forty-seven, she received a diagnosis. It was not the worst possible news, but it was not good. A small mass. Further testing needed. The word "concerning" appeared twice in the report.

She told her husband, her sister, her adult children. Then she lay awake at three in the morning, building a cathedral of catastrophe. *What if it's malignant? What if I can't work? What if I become a burden? What if I lose my hair and my dignity and my identity as a competent person?*

Each question produced another. The diagnosis was one sentence. The suffering she was creating in the dark bedroom was a novel she had written herself.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Then Sarah remembered the unbreakable center. Not a sentimental idea. A practical distinction. Her body might be threatened. Her career might change. Her appearance might change. But her judgment, her courage, her capacity to face reality and choose her response — those were still hers.

This did not erase her fear. It simply located her power. She could face the tests. She could speak honestly with her family. She could continue being a doctor and a mother and a wife, even if the script changed.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, the Unbreakable Me.** Write about a time something broke or ended for you. Then describe the part of you that stayed whole. What did you discover about yourself?

**Second, the Story Rewrite.** Take one difficult experience. Write two versions: one where you are a victim, and one where you are someone who can help others because of what you endured. Which do you want to carry?

**Third, the Dignity Inventory.** Over the last month, identify one moment when you kept your dignity under pressure. Write it down. Notice what allowed it.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The world will break things. Your body, your plans, your relationships, your assumptions. That is the nature of being alive. But you are more than the things that happen to you.

Inside you lives a center that can survive almost anything — if you refuse to give it away.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
