# Epictetus Audio Script — Elementary (Ages 5–10)
## Chapter 5: The Guest at the Banquet

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Gentle, adventurous, respectful of young listeners

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back, young philosopher. Today we are going to talk about one of the strangest and most important ideas in the world: nothing lasts forever. And that is okay.

Epictetus compared life to a banquet. A banquet is a big fancy dinner with lots of dishes and guests. Imagine you are invited to a feast. You did not earn the invitation. You did not cook the food. You did not build the house. You are simply a guest. And a good guest knows how to receive what is offered with gratitude and how to leave without complaining when the meal is over.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a girl named Sophia.

Sophia had a best friend named Maya. They did everything together. They sat together at lunch. They rode bikes after school. They had a secret handshake and a secret hideout under the old oak tree.

Then one day, Maya’s father got a new job in another city. Maya was moving away.

Sophia felt like the sky had fallen. She could not imagine school without Maya. She could not imagine recess without her. The secret hideout would be empty. The secret handshake would be forgotten.

She was angry at Maya’s father. She was angry at the world. She wanted to keep Maya forever.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Sophia’s grandmother sat with her on the porch.

"Tell me," her grandmother said, "was Maya ever yours to keep?"

Sophia frowned. "She was my best friend."

"Yes," her grandmother said. "But was she a person, or was she a possession?"

Sophia thought about that. "A person."

"And people belong to themselves, don't they?"

"Yes."

"Then Maya was a gift," her grandmother said. "A wonderful, precious gift. And gifts are not kept forever. They are enjoyed while we have them, and thanked for when they go."

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that everything in life is on loan. Your favorite toy. Your pet. Your friends. Even the people you love most. They are not yours to keep. They are gifts given for a time.

This idea can sound sad at first. But actually, it is the opposite. When you know something is a gift, you enjoy it more. You pay attention to it. You do not take it for granted.

Think of it like borrowing a library book. If you know you have to give it back, you read it more carefully. You do not shove it under your bed and forget about it.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three things to try this week:

**First, make a Gratitude Scan.** List ten things in your life right now that you would miss if they were gone. Do not write what you think you should be grateful for. Write what you would actually miss.

**Second, write a Loan Agreement.** Pick three things that matter to you — a person, a place, and a thing. Write them down as if they were on loan. How does that change how you treat them today?

**Third, write a Memento Mori Letter.** That phrase means "remember you will die." It sounds scary, but it just means: life is short, so pay attention. Write a letter from an older version of yourself. What would they tell you to stop worrying about? What would they tell you to enjoy?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The banquet of life is beautiful. The food, the friends, the laughter — it is all a gift. A good guest does not grab everything and refuse to leave. A good guest says thank you, enjoys the meal, and walks out the door with dignity when the feast is over.

That is what Epictetus wanted us to learn. Not to love less, but to hold more lightly.

See you next time, philosopher.

### [MUSIC FADES]
