# Epictetus Audio Script — Middle School (Ages 11–14)
## Chapter 5: The Guest at the Banquet

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Respectful, slightly more mature, still accessible

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to talk about something that sounds sad at first, but is actually one of the most freeing ideas Epictetus taught: nothing lasts forever.

Epictetus compared life to a banquet. A big, beautiful feast. You did not earn the invitation. You did not cook the food. You are simply a guest. And a good guest knows how to receive what is offered with gratitude and how to leave without complaining when the meal is over.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a boy named Ethan.

Ethan was thirteen, and his family had just moved to a new city. He missed his old friends. He missed his old room. He missed knowing where everything was and who everyone was.

In his new school, he made one good friend quickly. Her name was Aisha. She was funny, kind, and loved the same weird sci-fi books Ethan loved. For the first time since the move, Ethan felt like himself again.

Then, at the end of the school year, Aisha told him her family was moving to another state.

Ethan felt the ground shift beneath him. He had already lost one home. Now he was losing the friendship that had made the new place feel like home.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
His grandmother noticed he was quiet.

"Tell me about Aisha," she said.

Ethan told her. How much he would miss her. How unfair it felt. How he wished he could keep him from leaving.

"Was Aisha ever yours to keep?" his grandmother asked gently.

Ethan frowned. "She was my friend."

"Yes. But was she a possession, or a person?"

"A person."

"Then she was a gift," his grandmother said. "A wonderful gift. And gifts are not kept forever. They are enjoyed while we have them, and thanked for when they go."

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that everything in life is on loan. Friends, family, health, possessions, even our own bodies. We do not get to keep them forever. But that is not a reason to love them less. It is a reason to love them more carefully.

When you know something is temporary, you pay attention to it. You do not take it for granted. You do not postpone kindness.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, make a Gratitude Scan.** List ten things in your life right now that you would miss if they were gone. Not what you should be grateful for — what you would actually miss.

**Second, write a Loan Agreement.** Pick three things that matter to you: a person, a place, and a possession. Write them down as if they were on loan. How does that change how you treat them today?

**Third, write a Memento Mori Letter.** That phrase means "remember you will die." It sounds dark, but it just means: life is short, so pay attention. Write a letter from an older version of yourself. What would they tell you to stop worrying about? What would they tell you to enjoy?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The banquet of life is beautiful. The people, the moments, the small joys — they are all gifts. A good guest does not grab everything and refuse to leave. A good guest says thank you, enjoys the meal, and walks out the door with dignity when the feast is over.

That is what Epictetus wanted us to learn. Not to love less, but to hold more lightly.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
