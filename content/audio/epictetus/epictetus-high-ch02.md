# Epictetus Audio Script — High School (Ages 15–18)
## Chapter 2: The Circle You Control

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, philosophical, emotionally honest

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to examine the idea that made Epictetus famous: the dichotomy of control. Some things are up to us. Most things are not. The beginning of wisdom is learning the difference.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Picture a circle. Inside it, write what is genuinely yours: your judgments, your impulses, your desires, your aversions, your effort, your responses, your attention. Outside it, write what is not: your body, your property, your reputation, public office, other people’s actions, outcomes, the future.

This sounds simple. It is not easy.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a high school student named Marcus.

Marcus was sixteen, and he had spent the last three years building his identity around one thing: getting into a prestigious summer program. It would look good on college applications. It would impress his parents. It would prove, finally, that he was exceptional.

He worked for it. He took extra classes. He stayed up late. He networked with teachers and alumni. He wrote and rewrote his application essay.

He did not get in.

For days, Marcus was consumed by the rejection. He replayed every choice. Should he have applied earlier? Should he have asked someone else for a recommendation? Should he have written a safer essay? He obsessed over the other students who had gotten in. He imagined their futures unfolding while his stalled.

Then one evening, his mother asked a question that stopped him.

"Was the admissions committee inside your circle?"

Marcus stared at her. "No."

"Was their decision inside your circle?"

"No."

"Then why are you building your life on it?"

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have understood Marcus perfectly. The modern world pressures young people to identify with externals: test scores, college admissions, social media metrics, athletic rankings, romantic attention, future income. Each of these matters. None of them is a safe foundation for the self.

The Stoic move is not to stop caring. It is to stop staking your peace on what you cannot command.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, draw your Circle of Control.** Be ruthless. Inside: only what you can directly command. Outside: everything else. Notice how much smaller the inside is — and how much power it contains.

**Second, do the Worry Sort.** Take five current worries. For each, ask: inside or outside my circle? If outside, write one response you can control. If inside, write one small action.

**Third, keep a Daily Check-In.** For one week, before bed, ask: "Did I spend more energy today on what I control or on what I do not?" Just notice.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The circle of control is not a magic solution. It will not make rejection feel good or failure feel fair. But it will return to you the energy you have been spending on outcomes you cannot command.

And that energy is more valuable than you think.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
