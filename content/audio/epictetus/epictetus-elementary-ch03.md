# Epictetus Audio Script — Elementary (Ages 5–10)
## Chapter 3: The Unbreakable Center

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Gentle, adventurous, respectful of young listeners

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back, young philosopher. Today we are going to talk about something every person needs to know: how to keep going when something breaks.

Not just toys. Not just dishes. But hearts, hopes, and plans.

Epictetus taught that inside every person, there is a part that cannot be broken — unless we give it away. He called this part the Unbreakable Center.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a girl named Livia.

Livia was ten years old, and she had made something with her own hands that she loved more than anything else in her room. It was a small clay statue she had shaped in her mother’s pottery class. She had rolled the clay, carved the face, painted it with berry juice, and even made hair from frayed rope.

The statue was not perfect. One eye was bigger than the other. The arms were different lengths. The paint was chipped. But Livia called her Fortuna, and she loved her.

Fortuna sat on a shelf beside Livia’s bed. When Livia was scared, she whispered to Fortuna. When she was sad, she held Fortuna in her hands. The little statue seemed to hold pieces of Livia’s soul.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
One festival day, Livia’s cousins came to visit. The house was noisy and crowded. Everyone was laughing and eating. Livia took Fortuna down to show her cousins.

But while everyone was moving around the table, her cousin Marcus stepped backward. He did not see Fortuna on the floor. His foot came down. And crack — the little statue broke into pieces.

Livia froze. The head rolled one way. The legs rolled another. The painted face looked up at her from the stone floor.

For a moment, nobody said anything. Then Marcus mumbled, "Sorry," and reached for another piece of bread.

Livia felt like the floor had opened beneath her. She ran to her room and cried harder than she had cried in a long time. She cried not just because a statue broke, but because something inside her felt broken too.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
The next morning, Livia’s mother took her to see a philosopher named Epictetus.

He was sitting in a colonnade — a long porch with tall columns — leaning on his wooden staff. When Livia sat down, he did not give her a lecture. He just looked at her with kind eyes.

"Tell me about Fortuna," he said.

Livia told him everything. How hard she had worked. How much she loved the statue. How unfair it felt that it was gone.

Epictetus listened. Then he asked, "Is the part of you that made Fortuna gone too?"

Livia blinked. "No."

"Is the patience you used gone?"

"No."

"Is the love you put into her gone?"

Livia thought about it. "No."

"Then the statue broke," Epictetus said gently. "But the maker did not."

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that things outside us can be broken, lost, or taken away. Toys break. People move away. Plans change. Bodies get hurt. But the part of us that chooses, that loves, that creates, that endures — that part does not have to break.

This does not mean we do not feel sad when things go wrong. Of course we do. Livia was right to cry. Her statue was precious. But the sadness does not have to be the end of the story.

We can be sad about what broke and still remember that we are whole.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three things to try this week:

**First, find your Unbreakable Me.** Think of a time when something broke or ended for you. Maybe a toy. Maybe a friendship. Maybe a plan. Then write or draw about the part of you that stayed whole.

**Second, try the Story Rewrite.** Think of something hard that happened. Write two versions: one where the hard thing made you stuck, and one where you chose to keep going. Which one feels more like the real you?

**Third, practice comfort.** This week, when you see someone sad or hurt, choose kindness. Ask if they want to talk, or just sit with them. Notice how helping someone else can also help you feel stronger.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The world will break things. That is part of being alive. But you are more than the things that happen to you. Inside you lives a center that cannot be broken — unless you forget it is there.

So remember it. Especially on the hard days.

See you next time, philosopher.

### [MUSIC FADES]
