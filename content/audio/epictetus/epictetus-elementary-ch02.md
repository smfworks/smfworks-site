# Epictetus Audio Script — Elementary (Ages 5–10)
## Chapter 2: The Circle You Control

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Gentle, adventurous, respectful of young listeners

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back, young philosopher. Last time, we met Epictetus — a boy with a borrowed name who grew up to become one of the wisest teachers in the Roman Empire. Today, we are going to learn one of his most important ideas. It is called the Circle of Control. And once you see it, you will notice it everywhere.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Imagine you are drawing a picture. In the center, you draw a circle. Inside the circle, you write things like: my effort, my kindness, my honesty, my attention, my choices. Outside the circle, you write things like: the weather, other people’s moods, what grade someone else gets, whether it rains on the picnic, what the teacher decides, what happened yesterday.

Epictetus taught that most of our unhappiness comes from spending too much time and energy on things outside the circle. We worry about the weather. We worry about what other people think. We worry about things that already happened. And while we are worrying about those things, we have no energy left for the things we can actually do something about.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you a story about a girl named Livia.

Livia was nine years old, and she was having a terrible morning. She had studied hard for her math test. She had done her best. But when the teacher handed back the tests, Livia’s friend Claudia got a higher score. And Claudia had not even studied very hard.

Livia felt a hot, prickly feeling in her chest. It was not fair. She had worked so hard. She had stayed up late practicing. And now Claudia — who had been drawing cartoons during study time — had a better grade.

Livia spent the whole morning feeling angry. She did not hear the teacher’s instructions for the next assignment. She did not want to play at recess. She just sat there, crossing her arms, replaying the unfairness over and over in her mind.

At lunch, her grandfather asked what was wrong. Livia told him the whole story.

Her grandfather listened. Then he took a napkin and drew a circle.

"Inside this circle," he said, "are the things Livia can control. Outside are the things she cannot. Where does Claudia’s grade go?"

"Outside," Livia mumbled.

"Where does your effort go?"

"Inside."

"Where does your choice to be happy anyway go?"

Livia paused. "Inside?"

"Yes," her grandfather said. "And that is where your power lives."

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have liked Livia’s grandfather. He taught that we should not waste our energy trying to control things that are not up to us. We cannot control the wind. We cannot control other people’s choices. We cannot control what already happened.

But we can control our own thoughts, our own efforts, and our own responses.

This does not mean we do not care about things outside the circle. Of course we care if it rains on the picnic. Of course we care if a friend is sad. Of course we care if we do not do as well as we hoped. But we do not have to let those things steal our peace.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here is a secret: the more time you spend inside your circle, the stronger you become. It is like building a muscle. Every time you choose to focus on what you can control, you get a little stronger. Every time you let go of something outside your circle, you get a little freer.

Let me give you an example. Imagine you are playing a game. You cannot control whether your team wins. But you can control whether you try your best, whether you encourage your teammates, and whether you shake hands with the other team when it is over.

If you spend the whole game worrying about the score, you will not play very well. But if you focus on what you can do right now, you might actually help your team more.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
So here are three things to try this week:

**First, draw your own Circle of Control.** Get a big piece of paper and draw a circle. Inside, write everything you can control. Outside, write things you cannot control. Color the inside bright and warm. Put it somewhere you can see it.

**Second, play the Worry Sort.** When you feel worried or upset, ask yourself: "Is this inside my circle or outside my circle?" If it is outside, take a deep breath and let it go. If it is inside, ask, "What is one small thing I can do?"

**Third, practice the Daily Check-In.** Before bed, ask yourself, "Did I spend more energy today on things inside my circle or outside it?" Do not judge yourself. Just notice.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The Circle of Control is one of the most powerful ideas ever written down. It is also one of the hardest to practice. Even grown-ups forget it all the time. But the more you remember it, the calmer and stronger you become.

Because here is the truth: you cannot control the whole world. But you can control the place where you stand. And that is enough.

See you next time, philosopher.

### [MUSIC FADES]
