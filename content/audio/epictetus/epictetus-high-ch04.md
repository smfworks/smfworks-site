# Epictetus Audio Script — High School (Ages 15–18)
## Chapter 4: The Two Arrows

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, philosophical, emotionally honest

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to talk about one of the most precise and useful ideas in Stoicism: the two arrows. It is about the difference between the pain that life gives us and the suffering we add to it.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here is the image. Life shoots an arrow at you. You get sick. A relationship ends. You fail a test. Someone lies about you. That is the first arrow. It hurts. It is real. You did not ask for it.

But then, while you are sitting there, you often pick up the bow and shoot a second arrow at yourself. You catastrophize. You blame yourself. You replay the moment endlessly. You turn one bad event into a story about your whole life.

The second arrow is optional. And it usually hurts more than the first.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a student named Priya.

Priya was seventeen, and she had just sent a text message she instantly regretted. It was to a friend group, and in it she made a sarcastic comment about another girl — a comment that, when read by everyone, sounded crueler than she intended.

The first arrow was the text itself. The mistake. The awkwardness. The possibility that she had hurt someone.

But Priya did not stop there. She shot a second arrow: "I am a terrible person. Everyone is going to hate me. This will follow me forever. I always ruin things. I will never have real friends."

Within an hour, she had constructed a catastrophe. The actual event was one text message. The suffering she was experiencing was a novel she had written herself.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that we cannot always stop the first arrow. Bad things happen. Mistakes happen. Other people make choices we cannot predict.

But the second arrow — the meaning we attach, the story we tell, the spiral we choose — that is within our power.

Priya could not unsend the text. But she could stop adding to the damage by torturing herself. She could apologize sincerely. She could learn to pause before sending. She could decide that one mistake did not define her character.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, keep an Arrow Diary.** For five days, write down one difficult thing that happened. Then notice: did you shoot a second arrow? Rate it from 1 to 5. Look for patterns.

**Second, try the Five-Minute Rule.** When something upsetting happens, give yourself five minutes to feel it fully. Then ask, "What would I do next if I were not still reacting?"

**Third, practice apology over defense.** The next time you are in conflict, try saying what actually happened instead of protecting your pride. Notice how the conflict changes.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You cannot stop every first arrow. But you can usually lay down the bow. And when you do, you will be surprised how much less suffering remains.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
