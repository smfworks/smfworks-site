# Epictetus Audio Script — Elementary (Ages 5–10)
## Chapter 1: The Boy with No Name

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Gentle, adventurous, respectful of young listeners

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Hello, young philosopher. Today we're going to travel far back in time — more than two thousand years — to a place called Hierapolis. It's a city built on white cliffs, with steam rising from hot springs and travelers coming from all over the world. And in this city, there was once a baby boy. A very special baby. But nobody knew his real name.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
The city was beautiful. Water bubbled up from the earth, warm and mysterious. People said the water could heal sickness and take away pain. The cliffs looked like frozen waterfalls made of snow. But for all its beauty, Hierapolis was also a place where some people were owned by other people. And that is exactly what happened to our baby boy.

He did not get to keep his mother's name for him. He did not get a birthday party or a little wooden toy with his name carved into it. The name everyone called him was Epictetus. In Greek, that word meant "acquired." It was the kind of word you might use for a chair, or a horse, or a pair of sandals.

But a person is not a chair. A person is not a pair of sandals.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION, LIKE A HEARTBEAT]

**NARRATOR:**  
When Epictetus was still very small — maybe four or five years old — his master decided to move to Rome. Rome was the biggest city in the world. It had buildings so tall they seemed to touch the clouds. It had temples with golden roofs and a giant arena called the Colosseum where thousands of people gathered to watch games.

But Epictetus did not travel like a prince. He traveled in a covered wagon, bumping over mountains, crossing rivers on creaky boats, probably cold and confused and missing the only home he had ever known.

And his mother? She did not come.

We do not know why. Maybe she was sold to someone else. Maybe she died. Maybe she was simply left behind. But the little boy who had fallen asleep to her heartbeat was suddenly alone.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
In Rome, Epictetus became a slave in a grand house. The floors were marble. The walls were painted with bright pictures. Fountains splashed in the courtyard. But Epictetus slept on a thin mattress, ate simple food, and spent his days carrying water, cleaning floors, and running messages through crowded streets.

His master was a powerful man named Epaphroditus. One terrible day, Epaphroditus twisted Epictetus's leg until the bone broke.

Why? We do not know. Maybe Epictetus had dropped something. Maybe his master was angry about something else and took it out on the nearest person. Maybe there was no reason at all.

The pain must have been terrible. There were no doctors for enslaved children. No medicine. Someone probably wrapped the leg in strips of cloth and hoped for the best. When it healed, it healed crooked. For the rest of his life, Epictetus walked with a limp and leaned on a wooden staff.

But here is the amazing thing. Even as a child, in the middle of that terrible pain, Epictetus did something extraordinary. He looked at his master and said, calmly, "Didn't I tell you this would happen if you kept twisting it?"

He did not scream. He did not beg. He spoke the truth, even when his body was broken.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY, THEN SOFTENS]

**NARRATOR:**  
Years passed. Epictetus grew up. Somewhere along the way, he was freed. He could have done many things. He could have been a farmer or a soldier or a merchant. Instead, he became a philosopher.

He taught outdoors, under the sky, in a place called a colonnade — a long porch with columns. Students gathered around him. Some were rich. Some were poor. Some were old, some young. They came because Epictetus spoke with a kind of authority you cannot buy. He spoke from experience. He knew what it meant to have almost nothing and still be free inside.

He taught that your name, your body, your money, your job — none of these are the deepest part of you. The deepest part of you is the part that chooses. The part that decides what to believe. The part that decides how to respond when life is unfair.

That part, he said, nobody can take away.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
So what does this mean for you?

Think about your own name. Maybe you love it. Maybe someone teased you about it. Maybe it was passed down from a grandparent. Your name is part of your story, but it is not all of you.

Epictetus teaches us that who we are is deeper than what we are called. Deeper than what we own. Deeper than what happens to our bodies.

Let me say that again, because it is the heart of this whole story: **Who you are is deeper than what happens to you.**

A bully can take your lunch but not your courage. A storm can cancel your game but not your kindness. A bad day can make you sad but cannot reach the part of you that chooses how to respond.

### [SOFT MUSIC RETURNS]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three small things you can try this week:

**First, learn the sound of your own inner voice.** When something hard happens, notice what you say to yourself. Do you say, "This is impossible"? Or do you say, "I can handle this"? Try switching to a braver sentence.

**Second, notice what you cannot be forced to give.** Someone can make you clean your room, but they cannot make you hate doing it. Someone can take your toy, but they cannot take your imagination. Someone can be mean to you, but they cannot make you mean back.

**Third, ask a grown-up you trust:** "What is something hard that happened to you, and how did you stay strong inside?" Listen carefully. Every person has a little Epictetus inside them.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The next time someone hurts your feelings, or the world feels unfair, or you are afraid — remember the boy with the borrowed name. Remember that inside you lives a place no one can enter without permission. A place where you choose. A place where you are free.

That is the beginning of philosophy. And you just took the first step.

I will see you in the next episode.

### [MUSIC FADES]
