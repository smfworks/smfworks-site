# Epictetus Audio Script — Adult
## Chapter 5: The Guest at the Banquet

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, reflective, direct, no self-help padding

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are talking about one of the hardest ideas in Stoicism: everything is on loan. It sounds like a reason for sadness. Properly understood, it is a reason for attention.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus compared life to a banquet. You did not earn the invitation. You did not cook the meal. You did not build the hall. You are a guest. A good guest receives what is offered with gratitude and leaves without complaint when the feast is over.

The banquet includes your body, your possessions, your roles, your relationships, and your very life. None of them are permanent possessions. All of them are temporary gifts.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a man named Robert.

Robert was fifty-eight, and he had built a small business over thirty years. It was not a fortune, but it was his. He knew every employee, every customer, every invoice. The business was his identity, his proof that he had mattered.

Then a larger competitor moved into his market. Prices dropped. Customers left. Within two years, Robert had to sell what remained and close the doors.

He was not just losing a business. He was losing a self.

His daughter sat with him in the empty office on the last day.

"Was the business ever really yours to keep?" she asked.

Robert frowned. "I built it."

"Yes. But markets change. Customers choose. Competitors arrive. You created it, but you never controlled all the conditions that allowed it to exist."

"So what was it?" he asked.

"A gift," she said. "A long, complicated gift. And you used it well."

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have agreed. Treating something as a loan does not mean loving it less. It means holding it more honestly. When you know something is temporary, you do not postpone the important conversations. You do not take people for granted. You do not confuse your roles with your essence.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, the Gratitude Scan.** List ten things in your life right now that you would miss if they were gone. Include at least one person, one place, one ability, one ordinary daily pleasure, and one thing you usually complain about.

**Second, the Loan Agreement.** Pick three things that matter to you — a possession, a relationship, and a status. Write each down as if on loan. How does that change how you treat each one today?

**Third, the Memento Mori Letter.** Write a short letter to yourself from the perspective of someone near the end of life. What would they tell you to stop worrying about? What would they tell you to pay attention to?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The banquet of life is beautiful. The people, the work, the small joys — all gifts. A good guest does not grab everything and refuse to leave. A good guest says thank you, enjoys the meal, and walks out the door with dignity when the feast is over.

That is what Epictetus wanted us to learn. Not to love less, but to hold more lightly.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
