# Epictetus Audio Script — Middle School (Ages 11–14)
## Chapter 4: The Two Arrows

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Respectful, slightly more mature, still accessible

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to learn one of the most practical ideas Epictetus ever taught. It is called The Two Arrows. And it is about the difference between pain and suffering.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here is the image. Imagine you are walking through the woods, and an arrow hits your arm. That is the first arrow. It hurts. It is scary. You did not ask for it.

But then, while you are sitting there with the arrow in your arm, you start shooting a second arrow at yourself. You think: "This is the worst day of my life. Why does this always happen to me? I am so unlucky. My whole week is ruined. Nobody understands how bad this is."

The second arrow is the story you tell yourself about the first arrow. And here is the strange thing: the second arrow often hurts more than the first.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a girl named Maya.

Maya was twelve, and she had been preparing for a school play for two months. She had a small part, but she had worked hard on it. She knew her lines. She had helped paint the sets. She was excited.

On opening night, she forgot one of her lines. Not a big line. Just one sentence. But in the moment, it felt enormous. The audience did not even notice. Her scene partner covered for her smoothly. The play went on.

But Maya could not let it go. On the way home, she replayed the moment over and over. "I am so stupid. I ruined the whole play. Everyone thinks I am a terrible actor. I will never try out for anything again."

She had been hit by one small arrow — forgetting a line. But she had shot herself with a dozen more.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that we cannot always stop the first arrow. Bad things happen. Mistakes happen. People disappoint us. We get sick. We fail. Those are first arrows.

But the second arrow — the extra suffering we add — is optional. We do not have to make a bad situation worse by catastrophizing, blaming, or replaying it endlessly.

Maya’s mistake was real. Forgetting the line was embarrassing. But the play was not ruined. Nobody thought she was a terrible actor. And she did not have to swear off theater forever.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here is the practice. The next time something upsetting happens, try to notice: am I adding a second arrow? Am I turning one bad thing into a story about my whole life?

If you are, that is okay. It is a habit most of us have. But you can choose to set the second arrow down.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, keep an Arrow Diary.** For five days, write down one difficult thing that happened. Then notice: did you shoot a second arrow? Did you make it worse with thoughts like "always," "never," or "worst ever"?

**Second, try the Five-Minute Rule.** When something upsetting happens, give yourself five minutes to feel it fully. Then ask, "What would I do next if I were not still reacting?"

**Third, practice apology over defense.** The next time you are in a small conflict, try saying what actually happened instead of protecting your pride. See if the conflict gets smaller.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You cannot stop every first arrow. But you can lay down the bow. And when you do, you will be surprised how much lighter life feels.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
