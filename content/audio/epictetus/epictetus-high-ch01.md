# Epictetus Audio Script — High School (Ages 15–18)
## Chapter 1: The Boy with a Borrowed Name

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, philosophical, emotionally honest

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Hello and welcome. In this series, we are meeting a philosopher whose life was harder than most of his ideas let on. His name was Epictetus. He was born enslaved, had his leg broken by his owner, and still became one of the most respected teachers in the Roman Empire.

This is not a success story in the usual sense. It is a story about what remains when almost everything is taken away.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus was born in Hierapolis, a city built on white cliffs above mineral hot springs. The name we know him by — Epictetus — means "acquired." It is the kind of word used for property, not people. He did not choose it. He could not change it.

When he was still a child, his master moved to Rome. The journey was long and dangerous. And his mother did not come. We do not know why. History swallowed the reason whole.

In Rome, he served in the household of Epaphroditus, a wealthy and powerful man who had once been a secretary to Emperor Nero. The house was grand. Marble floors. Painted walls. Fountains in the courtyard. But Epictetus lived the life of a slave: invisible, vulnerable, expendable.

Then came the breaking.

Ancient historians record it with terrible brevity: Epaphroditus twisted Epictetus's leg until the bone snapped. There was no doctor for slaves. No anesthesia. The leg healed crooked. For the rest of his life, Epictetus walked with a limp and leaned on a staff.

But in that moment of agony, the boy did something that revealed the man he would become. He looked at his master and said, calmly, "Didn't I tell you this would happen if you kept twisting it?"

Not a scream. Not a plea. A statement of fact. Even in pain, some part of him remained free.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Years later, Epictetus was freed. He studied philosophy under Musonius Rufus. Eventually, he began teaching on his own, outdoors, in public colonnades. Students gathered — not because he was famous, but because he spoke from experience.

He taught that the deepest part of a person is not the body, not the name, not the social role. It is the reasoning, choosing self — what he called *prohairesis*. It is the part of you that judges impressions, directs desires, and decides how to respond to the world.

That part, he said, cannot be touched by anyone else unless you allow it.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
At your age, this idea is both thrilling and threatening. Thrilling because it promises a kind of freedom no one can take away. Threatening because it means you are responsible for what you do with your mind.

You cannot always choose your circumstances. You cannot choose your parents, your body, your history, the economic class you were born into, the mistakes other people make. But you can choose what to do with what you have been given.

Epictetus's question is still urgent: what part of you is truly yours, and what part have you handed over to forces you cannot control?

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three questions to sit with this week:

**First:** What is one external thing — approval, grades, appearance, status, relationships — that you have allowed to define too much of your worth?

**Second:** If that external thing disappeared tomorrow, what would remain? What part of you is not for sale, not up for a vote, not available to be taken?

**Third:** Who is someone in history or your own life who maintained dignity under constraint? What did they protect inside themselves?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus started with almost nothing: no freedom, no family, no name of his own. And yet he became one of the most respected teachers in the Roman Empire. How? By discovering that the most important thing about a person cannot be stolen.

That thing is inside you too.

Thanks for listening. I will see you in the next episode.
