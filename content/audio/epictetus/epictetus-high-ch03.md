# Epictetus Audio Script — High School (Ages 15–18)
## Chapter 3: The Unbreakable Center

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, philosophical, emotionally honest

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to talk about loss. Not the small losses, like a missed bus or a lost phone, but the losses that feel like they take pieces of us with them.

Epictetus taught that there is a part of us that does not have to break. He called it the reasoning, choosing self. We can think of it as the unbreakable center.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a girl named Livia.

Livia was seventeen, and she had spent years preparing for a swimming scholarship. Swimming was not just what she did; it was who she was. She woke before dawn for practice. She counted calories. She skipped parties. She had shaped her whole identity around the pool.

Then, during a meet, she injured her shoulder. The doctor's words were gentle but final: she would never compete at that level again.

Livia felt as if the floor of her life had collapsed. Who was she without swimming? What was the point of all those early mornings? What would she tell people? What would she do next?

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Her coach sat with her after practice one day. He did not give her a motivational speech. He just asked, "What did swimming build in you that the injury cannot reach?"

Livia thought. "Discipline. Courage. The ability to keep going when it hurts."

"Did the injury take those away?"

"No."

"Then you have not lost everything. You have lost one expression of who you are. Not the foundation."

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have said something similar. He taught that externals can be damaged, lost, or taken away. But the qualities we build inside — discipline, courage, honesty, kindness, the capacity to endure — belong to us in a different way.

This is not toxic positivity. Livia was right to grieve. The loss was real. The Stoics did not deny pain. They denied that pain has to destroy the self.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, find your Unbreakable Me.** Write about a time something broke or ended for you. Then describe the part of you that stayed whole. What did you discover?

**Second, try the Story Rewrite.** Take one difficult experience. Write two versions: one where it made you a victim, and one where it made you someone who could help others. Which do you want to carry?

**Third, practice comfort.** When you see someone sad or hurt, choose kindness. Notice how helping someone else changes the weight of your own pain.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
Things will break. Plans will fail. Bodies will fail. People will leave. That is the nature of being alive. But inside you lives a center that can survive almost anything — if you choose to protect it.

That is the unbreakable center. And it is yours.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
