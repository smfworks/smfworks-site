# Epictetus Audio Script — Adult
## Chapter 1: The Boy with a Borrowed Name

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, reflective, direct, no self-help padding

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Hello. This is a series for adults who want to take Epictetus seriously — not as a feel-good slogan, but as a demanding practical philosophy. If you are looking for consolation, you will find some. But you will also find challenge.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus was born in Hierapolis, probably around 50 CE. He was born enslaved. The name we know him by means "acquired" — the kind of term used for furniture or livestock. It was not his mother's name for him. It announced, every time it was spoken, that he belonged to someone else.

When he was still a child, his master moved to Rome. His mother did not come. The reason was not recorded. Maybe she died. Maybe she was sold. Maybe she was simply left behind.

In Rome, Epictetus served in the household of Epaphroditus, a powerful freedman who had been secretary to Emperor Nero. The house was grand. The life was not. Then came the breaking. Ancient sources say simply that Epaphroditus twisted Epictetus's leg until the bone broke. There was no proper treatment. The leg healed crooked. For the rest of his life, he walked with a limp and leaned on a staff.

In that moment of agony, the boy looked at his master and said: "Didn't I tell you this would happen if you kept twisting it?" Calm. Factual. Dignified. Even then, some part of him remained free.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Years later, Epictetus was freed. He studied under Musonius Rufus, then taught on his own in the colonnades of Nicopolis. Students came — senators, soldiers, merchants, philosophers. They came because he spoke with the authority of someone who had tested his ideas against real constraint.

His central teaching was simple and severe: the most important thing about you is not your body, your wealth, your status, or your reputation. It is your *prohairesis* — your reasoning, choosing self. That is the only thing no one can take without your cooperation.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
As an adult, you already know how easily we give this away. We identify with job titles, salaries, relationships, appearance, social standing, parental approval, political outcomes. We let externals become the foundation of the self. Then we are shocked when they shift and the ground falls out.

Epictetus is not asking you to stop caring. He is asking you to locate yourself more carefully. Your worth is not your performance. Your dignity is not your reputation. Your peace is not someone else's opinion.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, the External Audit.** List five things you currently believe are necessary for your happiness or self-worth. For each, ask: Is it fully within my power? What would remain of me if it were taken away?

**Second, the Tyrant Test.** Identify one internal voice that controls you through fear — fear of rejection, failure, irrelevance, or inadequacy. Write a paragraph describing the part of you it cannot touch unless you allow it.

**Third, the Historical Parallel.** Read about one person who kept dignity under extreme constraint. What internal resource made that possible? How did they refuse to let their oppressor define them?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus began with nothing: no freedom, no family, no name of his own. And yet he became one of the most respected moral teachers in antiquity. The reason is not inspirational poster material. It is the rigorous practice of placing the self where it cannot be stolen.

That place is inside you too.

Thanks for listening.
