# Epictetus Audio Script — Middle School (Ages 11–14)
## Chapter 2: The Circle You Control

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Respectful, slightly more mature, still accessible

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Last time, we met Epictetus, a boy born into slavery who discovered an inner freedom no one could take away. Today, we are going to learn the idea he is most famous for: the Circle of Control.

It is simple to understand and difficult to practice. But once you see it, you will notice it everywhere.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Picture a circle. Inside the circle, write everything that is up to you: your effort, your attitude, your kindness, your honesty, your attention, your choices. Outside the circle, write everything that is not up to you: other people’s opinions, the weather, the past, the future, outcomes, other people’s moods, the grade someone else gets.

Epictetus taught that most of our suffering comes from confusing the inside with the outside. We stake our happiness on things we cannot control. Then we exhaust ourselves trying to manage what was never ours to manage.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a student named Marcus.

Marcus was thirteen, and he had been working for weeks on a history project. He had researched, outlined, made a beautiful poster, and practiced his presentation until he could do it in his sleep. On the day of the presentation, he was ready.

But when his teacher called his name, the classroom projector would not work. No matter what Marcus tried, the screen stayed blank. The teacher apologized. The other students shifted in their seats. And Marcus had to give his entire presentation without his carefully prepared slides.

Afterward, he got a B. The teacher wrote: "Excellent content, but visual aids were missing."

Marcus was furious. He had worked harder than anyone. The projector failure was not his fault. The grade felt like a punishment for something beyond his control.

For days, he replayed the presentation in his mind. He imagined himself demanding a regrade. He imagined telling the teacher she was unfair. He imagined the other students secretly laughing at him.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Then one evening, his older sister asked what was wrong. Marcus told her the whole story.

She listened, then said, "Okay, but what part of this is actually in your control?"

Marcus blinked. "None of it. The projector broke."

"Right. The projector is outside your circle. The grade is outside your circle. But your response? That is inside. How are you going to use this?"

Marcus thought about it. He could write to the teacher calmly and explain what happened. He could ask for a chance to present again. He could learn how to make a presentation that does not depend on technology. He could decide that one B did not define him.

The anger did not disappear. But it stopped controlling him.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
That is the Circle of Control in action. It does not mean you stop caring about outcomes. It means you stop making your peace depend on them.

Here is the hard part: modern life is designed to pull you outside your circle. Social media shows you other people’s highlight reels and makes you compare your inside to their outside. Schools grade you on outcomes, not just effort. Parents and coaches sometimes focus on results. It is easy to believe that your worth is the same as your performance.

But it is not.

Your worth is inside the circle. Your effort is inside the circle. Your kindness, your honesty, your courage, your willingness to try again — all inside.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, draw your Circle of Control.** Get a big piece of paper. Inside, write what you can actually control. Outside, write what you cannot. Be honest. Notice how much smaller the inside is — and how much power it contains.

**Second, do the Worry Sort.** Take five things you are worried about right now. For each one, ask: inside or outside my circle? If it is outside, write one thing you CAN control about how you respond. If it is inside, write one small action.

**Third, keep a Daily Check-In.** For one week, before bed, ask yourself: "Did I spend more energy today on things inside my circle or outside it?" Just notice. Do not judge.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
The Circle of Control is not magic. It will not make your problems disappear. But it will give you back the energy you have been spending on things you cannot change.

And that energy is more powerful than you think.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
