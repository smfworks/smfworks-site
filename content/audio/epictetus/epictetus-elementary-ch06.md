# Epictetus Audio Script — Elementary (Ages 5–10)
## Chapter 6: Practice Makes Strong

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Gentle, adventurous, respectful of young listeners

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back, young philosopher. We have come to the final chapter of our journey with Epictetus. We have learned about the boy with no name, the circle you control, the unbreakable center, the two arrows, and the guest at the banquet.

But there is one more thing we need to talk about, and it might be the most important of all.

Philosophy is not a book you read once. It is a practice.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Imagine you wanted to learn to play the piano. You would not just read a book about pianos and then expect to play a concert. You would practice. Every day, you would sit down and play scales. Your fingers would hurt. You would make mistakes. Sometimes you would get frustrated. But slowly, over time, you would get better.

Becoming wise is the same.

Epictetus taught that philosophy is training. It is not enough to know the ideas. You have to live them. You have to practice them the way an athlete practices running or a musician practices scales.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a boy named Lucius.

Lucius was eleven years old, and he wanted to be brave. He had read about heroes and warriors and wise philosophers. He wanted to be like them.

So he decided to practice courage. Not by doing one big brave thing, but by doing small brave things every day.

The first day, he spoke up in class when he knew the answer. The second day, he told the truth even though it meant getting in trouble. The third day, he tried a new food that looked weird. The fourth day, he apologized to a friend he had hurt.

None of these things were dramatic. None of them would be in a hero movie. But each one made Lucius a little braver. Each one taught him that courage is not the absence of fear. Courage is doing something even when you are afraid.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus said that if you want to be good at something, you have to practice it. If you want to be patient, practice patience. If you want to be kind, practice kindness. If you want to be brave, practice courage.

It sounds simple because it is. But simple is not the same as easy.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three ways to practice this week:

**First, make a Seven-Day Training Plan.** Pick one of the ideas from this booklet — the circle, the unbreakable center, the two arrows, or the banquet. Try it every day for a week. Keep a small log of what happens.

**Second, make a Trigger List.** Write down five things that usually make you react strongly. For each one, write a sentence you can say to yourself in the moment. Keep the sentences short. Carry them with you.

**Third, make a Personal Philosophy Card.** On an index card, write five rules for yourself inspired by Epictetus. Carry it for two weeks. Then revise it based on what you actually needed.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You have done something wonderful by listening to these stories. You have met a man who lived two thousand years ago and discovered that his wisdom is still alive today.

But the story is not over. The most important part is what you do next.

Will you practice? Will you come back to these ideas when life gets hard? Will you be the kind of person who chooses wisdom, even in small moments?

I hope so. Because the world needs more young philosophers like you.

This is George, wishing you courage, wisdom, and an unbreakable center.

Goodbye, philosopher.
