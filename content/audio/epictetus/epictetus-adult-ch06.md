# Epictetus Audio Script — Adult
## Chapter 6: Practice Makes Strong

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, reflective, direct, no self-help padding

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. We have come to the final chapter of our journey with Epictetus. We have explored the borrowed name, the circle of control, the unbreakable center, the two arrows, and the guest at the banquet.

But knowing these ideas is only the beginning. Philosophy is practice.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that philosophy is not a subject for the library. It is training for life. You do not become temperate by reading about temperance. You become temperate by practicing it when you are provoked. You do not become courageous by admiring courage. You become courageous by acting when you are afraid.

This is not dramatic. It is daily. It is mundane. It is the slow wearing of wisdom into the soul through repeated choice.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a man named James.

James was forty-nine, and he had spent years reacting badly to criticism at work. Feedback meetings made him defensive. He would leave them angry, then spend hours composing mental arguments. His team had learned to soften their critiques, which meant he was not really hearing them.

He decided to practice the pause. For one month, in any meeting where he felt criticized, he would count to three before responding. He would ask a clarifying question before defending himself.

It felt artificial at first. He failed often. But slowly, the pattern changed. One day, a direct report said, "I think the client presentation missed the mark." The old James would have argued. The new James asked, "What specifically felt off?"

The conversation that followed was honest and useful. Nothing heroic. But something real had shifted.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, the Seven-Day Training Plan.** Pick one idea from this series. Try it every day for a week. Keep a brief log. Notice how the practice changes your attention.

**Second, the Trigger List.** Identify five situations that usually provoke a strong reaction. For each, write a short sentence you can say to yourself in the moment. Carry them on a card.

**Third, the Personal Philosophy Card.** On an index card, write five Epictetus-inspired rules for yourself. Carry it for two weeks. Then revise it based on what you actually needed.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You have done something important by listening to these stories. You have met a man who lived two thousand years ago and discovered that his wisdom is still alive today.

But the story is not over. The most important part is what you do next.

Will you practice? Will you return to these ideas when life gets hard? Will you be the kind of person who chooses wisdom, even in small, unnoticed moments?

I hope so. Because the world needs more people like you.

This is George, wishing you courage, wisdom, and an unbreakable center.

Goodbye, philosopher.
