# Epictetus Audio Script — Middle School (Ages 11–14)
## Chapter 3: The Unbreakable Center

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Respectful, slightly more mature, still accessible

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are going to talk about something every person faces sooner or later: loss. Not just losing a game or losing a phone, but losing something that felt like part of who you were.

Epictetus taught that inside every person, there is a center that does not have to break. He called it the reasoning, choosing self. We might call it the unbreakable center.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a girl named Livia.

Livia was fourteen, and she had spent the entire summer building a robot. It was for a school competition, and she had poured everything into it: late nights, weekends, allowance money for parts, hours of YouTube tutorials. She had designed it herself, soldered the circuits, written the code, painted the frame.

The robot was not just a project. It was proof that she could do hard things. It was the first thing she had ever built entirely on her own.

The night before the competition, her little brother accidentally knocked it off the table. The frame cracked. A motor tore loose. The whole thing was ruined.

Livia stood in the doorway of her room and stared at the pieces. She felt hollow. All that work. All that time. Gone.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Her father found her there an hour later, still not moving.

"I know it hurts," he said. "But tell me: are you gone too?"

Livia looked at him. "What?"

"The robot broke. That is real. But did the breaking of the robot break you? Or are you still here?"

"I am still here," she whispered.

"What else is still here?"

Livia thought. "The things I learned. The late nights. The mistakes I figured out. The fact that I finished it once."

"Those things," her father said, "cannot be knocked off a table."

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have said the same thing. He taught that externals — things outside us — can always be damaged, lost, or taken away. But the part of us that learns, that creates, that endures, that chooses — that part does not have to break.

This is not about pretending you are not sad. Livia was right to be sad. Her robot was important. The Stoics were not robots themselves. They felt grief, disappointment, and fear. But they did not let those feelings destroy their sense of who they were.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
The unbreakable center is the part of you that survives loss. It is the part that says, "This hurts, but I am still here." It is the part that can start again. It is the part that knows you are more than any one achievement, possession, or relationship.

Here are three practices for this week:

**First, find your Unbreakable Me.** Write about a time when something broke or ended for you. Then describe the part of you that stayed whole. What did you discover about yourself?

**Second, try the Story Rewrite.** Think of something hard that happened. Write two versions: one where the event made you stuck, and one where you chose to keep going. Which version feels more like who you want to be?

**Third, practice comfort.** This week, when you see someone sad or hurt, choose kindness. Notice how helping someone else can make your own pain feel lighter.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
Things will break. Plans will fail. People will leave. That is part of being human. But you are more than the things that happen to you. Inside you lives a center that can survive almost anything.

That is the unbreakable center. And it is yours.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
