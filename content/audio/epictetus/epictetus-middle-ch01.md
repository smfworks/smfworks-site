# Epictetus Audio Script — Middle School (Ages 11–14)
## Chapter 1: The Boy with No Name

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Respectful, slightly more mature, still accessible

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Hello and welcome. In this series, we are journeying back more than two thousand years to meet one of the most remarkable philosophers who ever lived: Epictetus. His name is strange. His story is stranger. And his ideas might just change how you see yourself.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus was born in a city called Hierapolis, built on white cliffs above hot springs. Steam rose from cracks in the earth. Travelers came from across the Mediterranean to bathe in water they believed could heal sickness and ease pain.

But Epictetus was not born to travelers or merchants. He was born enslaved. In the Roman world, children born to enslaved people belonged to the master's household. They did not get birth certificates. They did not keep their own names.

The name the world knows him by — Epictetus — is not a name his mother chose. It means "acquired." It is the kind of word a master uses when he buys a new chair, a horse, or a person.

Think about that for a moment. Every time someone said his name, they were reminding him that he was someone else's property.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
When he was very young, his master decided to move to Rome. Imagine the journey: weeks bumping over mountains, crossing rivers on creaky ferries, sleeping in unfamiliar places. And his mother did not come. We do not know why. Maybe she died. Maybe she was sold. Maybe she was simply left behind.

The boy who had known only one home was suddenly heading to the largest city in the world.

In Rome, Epictetus served in the household of a wealthy and powerful man named Epaphroditus. The house had marble floors, painted walls, fountains in the courtyard. But Epictetus lived the life of a slave: carrying water, cleaning floors, running messages, standing silently in corners.

And then came the worst day.

Ancient historians record it with terrible coldness: Epaphroditus twisted Epictetus's leg until it broke. We do not know why. Perhaps Epictetus had dropped something. Perhaps his master was angry. Perhaps there was no reason at all.

There was no doctor for slaves. No pain medicine worth the name. The leg healed crooked. For the rest of his life, Epictetus walked with a limp and leaned on a wooden staff.

But in that moment of agony, the boy did something extraordinary. He looked at his master and said, calmly, "Didn't I tell you this would happen if you kept twisting it?"

Think about the strength that takes. To be in the middle of unbearable pain and still speak the truth. To be powerless in body and still powerful in mind.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Years later, Epictetus was freed. He could have done anything. He chose philosophy.

He taught outdoors, in public colonnades, where anyone could listen. Students gathered — first a few, then dozens, then hundreds. Word spread across the Roman Empire: a man who had been enslaved, whose leg was crooked, spoke with quiet authority about the one thing that could not be stolen.

The deepest part of you, he taught, is not your body. It is not your name. It is not your money or your status or your family. The deepest part of you is the part that chooses. The part that judges. The part that decides how to respond when life is unfair.

He called this part *prohairesis* — the reasoning, choosing self.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
So what does this mean for you?

Maybe nobody has ever twisted your leg. But you have probably felt powerless. Maybe someone spread a rumor about you. Maybe a teacher treated you unfairly. Maybe your family is going through something hard. Maybe you have been told, directly or indirectly, that your worth depends on your grades, your appearance, your popularity, or your performance.

Epictetus would say: those things are real, but they are not the deepest part of you.

The deepest part of you is the part that chooses how to respond. The part that can be hurt but not destroyed. The part that remains free even when everything else is constrained.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three questions to sit with this week:

**First:** What is something outside yourself that you have allowed to define your worth? A grade? A social media following? A parent's approval? A friend's opinion?

**Second:** If that thing disappeared tomorrow, what would remain of you? What parts of you are not for sale, not up for a vote, not available to be taken?

**Third:** Who is someone in your life — real or historical — who kept their inner freedom under pressure? What did they do that you admire?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus started with almost nothing: no freedom, no family, no name of his own. And yet he became one of the most respected teachers in the Roman Empire. How? By discovering that the most important thing about a person cannot be stolen.

That thing is inside you too.

Thanks for listening. I will see you in the next episode.
