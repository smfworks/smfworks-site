# Epictetus Audio Script — High School (Ages 15–18)
## Chapter 6: Practice Makes Strong

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, philosophical, emotionally honest

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. We have come to the final chapter of our journey with Epictetus. We have explored the borrowed name, the circle of control, the unbreakable center, the two arrows, and the guest at the banquet.

But these ideas are useless unless they become practice.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that philosophy is not an academic subject. It is training. It is the art of living well. And like any art, it must be practiced to be real.

You do not become courageous by thinking about courage. You become courageous by acting courageously when you are afraid. You do not become patient by admiring patience. You become patient by practicing it when you are impatient. You do not become free by reading about freedom. You become free by choosing freely in the moments when you most want to surrender.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a student named Noah.

Noah was sixteen, and he had a temper. He reacted quickly to criticism, especially from his father. Every conversation seemed to become an argument. He hated it, but he did not know how to stop.

He decided to practice the pause. For one month, every time he felt the heat rise in his chest, he would count to five before speaking. It sounded silly. It felt mechanical. For the first two weeks, he failed more often than he succeeded.

But slowly, the pause began to appear. One evening, his father made a comment about Noah's grades. The old Noah would have exploded. The new Noah counted to five. In that pause, he saw that his father was not attacking him. He was afraid for him.

Noah said, "I know you are worried. I am working on it."

It was not a movie moment. His father just nodded. But something shifted. The argument that always happened did not happen. And Noah felt, for the first time, that he was not a prisoner of his own reactions.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
That is practice. Small choices, repeated, until they become character.

Here are three ways to practice this week:

**First, make a Seven-Day Training Plan.** Pick one idea from this series. Try it every day for a week. Keep a brief log. Notice how the practice changes your attention.

**Second, make a Trigger List.** Identify five situations that usually make you react strongly. For each one, write a short sentence you can say to yourself in the moment. Carry them on a card.

**Third, make a Personal Philosophy Card.** On an index card, write five Epictetus-inspired rules. Carry it for two weeks. Then revise it based on what you actually needed.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You have done something significant by listening to these stories. You have met a man who lived two thousand years ago and discovered that his wisdom is still alive today.

But the story is not over. The most important part is what you do next.

Will you practice? Will you come back to these ideas when life gets hard? Will you be the kind of person who chooses wisdom, even in small, unnoticed moments?

I hope so. Because the world needs more people like you.

This is George, wishing you courage, wisdom, and an unbreakable center.

Goodbye, philosopher.
