# Epictetus Audio Script — Adult
## Chapter 4: The Two Arrows

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Mature, reflective, direct, no self-help padding

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back. Today we are examining one of the most precise tools in Stoic practice: the distinction between the first arrow and the second arrow.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
The image comes from the Buddhist tradition, but Epictetus uses the same logic. Life shoots an arrow at you. You get sick. You lose a job. A relationship ends. Someone lies about you. That is the first arrow. It hurts. It is real. You did not choose it.

Then, while you are sitting there, you often shoot a second arrow at yourself. You catastrophize. You blame. You replay. You build a story about what the event means for your entire life.

The second arrow is optional. And it is usually the one that does the most damage.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Let me tell you about a man named David.

David was forty-four, a project manager at a mid-sized tech company. He had spent months preparing a presentation for the executive team. It was his chance to be noticed, to secure a promotion, to finally prove he belonged at the table.

The morning of the presentation, the executives were distracted. One of them took a call. Another checked email. David's carefully constructed argument landed flat. By the end, he knew he had not made the impression he wanted.

The first arrow was the failed presentation. Disappointing. Embarrassing. Costly.

But David kept shooting. "I am not leadership material. I never will be. Everyone saw through me. I have wasted fifteen years in this industry. My wife will be disappointed. My kids will sense I am a failure. I am going to be stuck in this job until they lay me off."

By midnight, he had turned one bad hour into a verdict on his entire life.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus would have recognized this immediately. The first arrow is life. The second arrow is judgment. We cannot always stop the first. But we can refuse to keep loading the bow.

David could not redo the presentation. But he could stop adding to it. He could analyze what went wrong without turning it into self-destruction. He could ask for feedback. He could prepare better next time. He could decide that one bad hour did not define his career.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three practices for this week:

**First, the Arrow Diary.** For five days, write down one difficult thing that happened. Then rate, from one to five, how much you added to it with catastrophizing, blame, or replay. Look for patterns.

**Second, the Five-Minute Rule.** When something upsetting happens, give yourself five minutes to feel it fully. Then ask, "What would I do next if I were not still reacting?"

**Third, Apology vs. Defense.** Think of a current conflict. Write two responses: one defensive, one calm and honest. Which is harder to send? Which is more likely to improve the situation?

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
You cannot stop every first arrow. But you can lay down the bow. And when you do, you will find that much of your suffering was never required.

Thanks for listening. See you next time.
