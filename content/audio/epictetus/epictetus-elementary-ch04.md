# Epictetus Audio Script — Elementary (Ages 5–10)
## Chapter 4: The Two Arrows

**Voice:** George — Warm, Captivating Storyteller  
**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Tone:** Gentle, adventurous, respectful of young listeners

---

### [OPENING MUSIC: soft strings, something ancient and warm]

**NARRATOR:**  
Welcome back, young philosopher. Today we are going to learn one of the most useful ideas Epictetus ever taught. It is called The Two Arrows. And once you understand it, you will understand why some pains feel much bigger than they need to.

### [SOFT CHORD]

**NARRATOR:**  
Imagine you are walking through the woods, and suddenly — ouch! — an arrow hits your arm. It hurts. It is scary. You did not ask for it. That is the first arrow.

Now imagine that, while you are sitting there with the arrow in your arm, you start thinking: "This is the worst day of my life! Why does this always happen to me? I bet nobody else ever gets hit by arrows. Now I will miss the party. My whole summer is ruined. I am so unlucky."

Those thoughts are the second arrow. And here is the strange thing: the second arrow hurts more than the first one.

### [GENTLE PERCUSSION]

**NARRATOR:**  
Epictetus taught that life will shoot us with first arrows all the time. We get sick. We fall down. Someone is mean to us. We make a mistake. A friend moves away. These things hurt, and they are not our fault.

But the second arrow — the extra suffering we add — that one is up to us.

Let me tell you about a boy named Marcus.

Marcus was eight years old, and he loved soccer. He had been practicing for weeks for the big game. But on the morning of the match, he woke up with a sore throat and a fever. His mother said he could not go.

"You are too sick," she said. "You will have to stay home and rest."

Marcus was devastated. The first arrow was missing the game. That was hard enough.

But then Marcus started shooting second arrows at himself. "This is not fair!" he thought. "Everyone else gets to play. My team will lose because of me. My coach will be disappointed. I will probably never get another chance like this. I am the unluckiest person in the world."

By the time his mother brought him soup, Marcus was not just sick. He was miserable.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Then Marcus remembered something Epictetus had said: "It is not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters."

He took a deep breath. Missing the game was still sad. But did his team really need him to win? No. Would he get other chances to play? Yes. Was being sick his fault? No.

Marcus closed his eyes and pictured the second arrow dissolving into smoke. He could not stop the first arrow. But he did not have to keep shooting himself.

### [MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here is the wisdom: pain is part of life, but suffering is optional. Something bad happens. That is one thing. Then we add worry, anger, blame, and catastrophizing. That is the second thing. We cannot always stop the first, but we can usually stop the second.

This does not mean pretending everything is fine. If you are sad, be sad. If you are disappointed, be disappointed. But you do not have to make it worse by telling yourself a terrible story about it.

### [PAUSE]

**NARRATOR:**  
Here are three things to try this week:

**First, keep an Arrow Diary.** For five days, write down one hard thing that happened each day. Then notice: did you shoot a second arrow? Did you make it worse with thoughts like "always," "never," or "worst day ever"?

**Second, try the Five-Minute Rule.** When something upsetting happens, give yourself five full minutes to feel it. Then ask, "What would I do next if I were not still reacting?"

**Third, practice apologizing instead of defending.** The next time you are in a small conflict, try saying what actually happened instead of trying to protect your pride. See if the conflict gets smaller.

### [CLOSING MUSIC]

**NARRATOR:**  
Life will shoot first arrows. That is unavoidable. But you do not have to pick up the bow and shoot the second one.

That choice is yours. And that is where your power lives.

See you next time, philosopher.

### [MUSIC FADES]
